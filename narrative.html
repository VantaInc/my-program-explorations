06 — Vision considerations

The bigger structural questions

Before we can build program intelligence, we need to resolve what the foundation looks like. These aren't feature decisions — they're architectural ones that shape everything downstream.

Untangle Home and Onboarding

SOC-2 guide, My Work, and My Program all blur together as entry points. They need to become distinct, intentional experiences.

Right now, new users land in a product that's trying to be a guide, a dashboard, a task list, and a home all at once. The result is none of them. We need to make a clear decision about what each surface is for — and who it's for.

SOC-2 Guide
Onboarding and framework setup. Temporal — it gets you to live, then gets out of the way.
My Work
Task and evidence management. Operational — the daily grind for practitioners and contributors.
My Program
Program-level intelligence. Strategic — for managers and leads who need the signal, not the noise.

These three can coexist — but only if they have clear, non-overlapping jobs. The friction today comes from each one trying to serve all users at all stages.

Home as a dynamic, user-aware surface

Home shouldn't be the same for a first-day contributor and a seasoned program lead. It should know who you are.

A true home experience adapts to user type, role, tenure, and context. What a contributor sees on day 3 is completely different from what a program manager sees heading into audit week. The surface shape-shifts — not in a jarring way, but in a way that makes every person feel like it was built for them.

New user
Guided, progressive, milestone-driven. Gets them to value fast.
Contributor
Task-focused, quick-action oriented. Get in, get it done.
Program lead
Intelligence-first. Status, signals, what needs a decision.
Executive
Digest view. One number, one risk, one ask.

Modernize the nav — ScoSeg integration

The nav is currently a static list. It should be a dynamic, context-aware surface — and ScoSeg needs to live inside it, not beside it.

ScoSeg today feels like a separate mode you switch into. The opportunity is to make scope and segmentation a first-class nav concept — something that's always present, always shaping what you see, rather than a separate flow you opt into.

A modernized nav understands what you're currently focused on. If you're in audit prep mode, the nav surfaces audit-relevant items. If you're in a vendor review, vendor-related tools come forward. The nav becomes a context layer, not just a link list.

Left nav ↔ Right canvas — the architecture

The most important structural idea: the nav operates independently from the workspace. This is the unlock for flexibility, focus modes, and scale.

Today the nav and the content are coupled — the nav points to a page, the page fills the canvas, full stop. The vision decouples them. The left nav becomes a persistent context layer. The right canvas becomes a composable workspace that can hold any combination of views, panels, and tools.
